What is Imposter Syndrome?

Impostor syndrome is a psychological pattern where individuals doubt their abilities and fear being exposed as frauds, despite their accomplishments. It was first described by psychologists Clance and Imes in a 1978 paper. Since then, numerous studies and resources have explored this phenomenon, including books like "The Secret Thoughts of Successful Women" by Valerie Young and "The Impostor Phenomenon" by Clance. TED Talks and Valerie Young's website also offer valuable insights and strategies to overcome it. Impostor syndrome can affect anyone and is characterized by self-doubt and attributing success to external factors, rather than one's own abilities. Recognizing it is the first step toward building self-confidence.

General Population: A review published in the International Journal of Behavioral Science (Sakulku & Alexander, 2011) suggests that about 70% of individuals experience impostor syndrome at some point in their lives.

Students: In a study published in the Journal of Multicultural Counseling and Development (Cokley et al., 2013), it was reported that approximately 66% of college students experience impostor syndrome. Another study published in the Journal of College Student Development (Bernard et al., 2002) found that 70% of college students had experienced impostor syndrome.

Professionals: A survey conducted by the International Journal of Behavioral Science found that around 30% of professionals experience impostor syndrome.
